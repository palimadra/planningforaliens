Web design is hard.

I am not a designer, I'm a developer. I recently decided my projects would benefit from more thoughtful, informed design so I'm learning a few design skills. I spent the last two weeks reading hundreds of articles on Smashing magazine and .Net magazine. I bought and read Bootstrapping Design and The Elements of Typographic Design. Then I put a few of my newly acquired skills to use redesigning this blog.

The result is what you are looking at now. It's fully responsive and works on every device and browser I could think of to test, It even works in IE6 and IE7. It just falls back to the mobile version. It took me five days, which took me completely by surprise. Building a website for every possible screen and browser is not a simple task. And for me at least I couldn't separate code and design, I had to do both at the same time. I can't imagine creating a psd for every breakpoint -- how would you even figure out what the breakpoints are? It's a brave new world out there and I can't imagine working on a team with designers that can't code.

The rest of this post is a walkthrough of my design process. If you're new to responsive web design this might be a good place to start. My blog is completely open source and available on Github. And while I'm new to design I'm not new to front-end development and I love helping people learn about programming. So get in touch on Twitter if you have any questions. But honestly the code is pretty straightforward -- nothing fancy. This was mainly an exercise in learning a little more about design.

Typography first.

This is a blog, so readability should be the first thing I think about when designing, and that means typography.
