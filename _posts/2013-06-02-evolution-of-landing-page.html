I love Gordon Ramsey's Kitchen Nightmares -- the old, BBC version, not the over-edited, American reality tv show. The best part about the show, and really the central tension that holds it together, is the conflict between Ramsey and restaurateur.

I like to yell at the screen:

"Why don't you just do what he says? He's an expert!"

"You asked to be on the show, you asked for advice from the expert, now just do exactly what he says!"

I stumbled into a Kitchen Nightmares situation last week.

I forked over a decent chunk of my "start my own thing" stash to fly to Philadelphia for BaconBiz Conference. (Restaurateur admits he needs help). At the end of the conference Patrick McKenzie suggested doing a few teardowns. (Restaurateur sees an opportunity for free consulting). I raised my hand and was selected. (Restaurateur gets selected for the show). The expert panel proceeded to dismantle my landing page. (Restaurateur ignores advice)?

Gordon Ramsey's producers would have hated me because there was no tension. I just sat there and frantically scribbled down what they were saying into my official BaconBiz notebook. And then, after flying home and deciphering my scrawl, I made a shiny new landing page.

Here's how I did it.

"Say why they want the thing sooner." -- Brennan Dunn

You have to read half of the original landing page before you know what's in my book. And if you miraculously cut a swathe through the jungle of shitty writing between you and this important information, you are rewarded with limp and flacid copy. We'll come back to this.

The first edit I made was to move the section about the book to the very top. I also added a header, "What you'll get out of this book" to make it even more obvious. Potential readers should have no trouble figuring out what's in it for them.

"Climb the ladder of abstraction." -- Amy Hoy

I didn't know what this meant when she said it but I wrote it in my notebook. Later I googled around and discovered Writing Tools: 50 Essential Strategies for Every Writer. Tool number 22 is "Climb up and down the ladder of abstraction." I'm not sure this is what Amy was referring to, but it's useful in any case.

Good copy moves strongly from concrete to abstract. Anything in the middle, between abstract and concrete, is in the danger zone.

Here's an example of what not to do from my old landing page.

"Finally understand how to use code in your design process."

Here is the new version.

"How to use the new flexbox module to get exactly the layout you want."

"How to use css combinators to style elements with surgical precision."

WTF is a "design process?" Sounds like something you would find burried in the middle of some corporate PowerPoint deck.

Maybe the reader doesn't know about the flexbox module, but it's a real thing they can use to get exactly the layout they want. This is much more useful than "code" they can use in their "design process."

"Finally I can strangle Photoshop." -- Amy Hoy
"Cut a step out and get my work done faster." -- Nathan Barry

I made some major structural changes that I can't pin to one piece of advice. After the teardown I was left with the impression that it wasn't clear what problem my book was solving. Amy and Nathan threw out some suggestions and since they are designers I listened carefully. But they weren't saying anything I hadn't heard before, and I didn't think just throwing their suggestions onto the landing page would help.

I spent most of my flight home to Chicago thinking about my "pain" problem. It seemed to me that pain and problem weren't the same thing, that pain is a result of a problem and I had conflated the two. Feeling guilty about struggling to design in the browser is not the problem, it's something else.

After the conference a friend sent me a link to something Brennan wrote a while ago. The important bit is the section "Marketers have already blazed the trail" which contains an outline for a landing page. I decided to use it as a framework for my landing page.

  • Pain -- Pain flows from the problem. The root problem is web designers don't have a tool that is just for them. This causes all kinds of pain as they are forced to jumpt through hoops with tools like Photoshop.
  • Solution -- One solution is to dive into writing the markup and CSS for the page and use Photoshop less.
  • Offer -- I wrote a book to show designers how to do this.
  • Objections -- Some designers may object to this solution because it's tough to think creatively while writing code. My answer to them is if they learn the right tools the technology will get out of the way.

Do you see what just happened? The first half of my old landing page just moved way down into the objections section.

"Make the headlines readable all the way down." -- Amy Hoy

Amy's landing page for Charm is very long. But if you are just skimming you can read only the headlines and it still makes sense.

I took the same approach. If you just read the bold bits it still makes sense.

"Short pages convert better for collecting emails." -- Nathan Barry

Verb + Benefit [+ immediacy] -- Patrick McKenzie
