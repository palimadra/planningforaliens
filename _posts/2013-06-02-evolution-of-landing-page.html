I am a developer, not a designer

When my landing page hit the big screen at the conference the designers in the audience and on the panel recoiled in horror. There were myriad problems.

  • The color blue is low contrast on white. Plus people will think it's a link.
  • The white text in the black box is too much contrast and uncomfortable to read.
  • Get rid of the black box.
  • The word "Sketch" needs to be the same color as the rest of the text. Otherwise it reads like: "Write css as fast as you can. SKETCH."
  • Too much spacing around the sign up form.
  • Point out somewhere that you are not a designer.

I think I've fixed most of these problems. I eliminated the blue text and the black box. There is much less space around the signup form, (in both locations). I also played with the spacing of the headers and paragraphs.

The new design is pretty boring but it's job is to be good enough to get out of the way of a potential reader for my book. There are so many ways it could look better, but it's good enough.

"Climbing the ladder of abstraction"

Of course this is just an hypothesis. But it is based on research that I did before even picking a topic for my book. Here are a few examples of articles I read that pointed me to this problem:

  • I can't design in the browser
  • Designing in the browser is not the answer
  • What to do when you can't design in the browser
  • Keep it creative
  • Chris Coyier can't design in the browser either

These articles, and especially the comments, heavily influenced the copy of the first three points on my original landing page, which are:

  • You've tried designing in the browser but it kills your creativity.
  • You just can't get into the flow when you are coding.
  • You feel a little guilty. All the awesome people in our industry design in the browser.

The confusion

After all of that research and painstakingly choosing my copy, imagine my surprise when the panel couldn't figure out what problem I was solving with my book.

After years of making landing pages for various wacky ideas and getting pummeled with brutal feedback on Reddit and Hacker News you'd think I'd have a tolerance for this sort of thing. I mean, I no longer take it personally and I certainly don't get defensive, but quite frankly I was downright annoyed with myself that the benefit of my book wasn't extremely obvious. How am I not a freaking expert at landing pages by now? Arg.

But of course if it's not obvious, it's not obvious, no matter how much research I did. So, how do I fix this? How do I rectify all of the research I did with this expert feedback?

Climbing down the ladder of abstraction

At some point in the flurry of comments Amy said, "you need to climb down the ladder of abstraction." At about the same time Brennan said, "say why they want the thing sooner" (which is ironically a less abstract way of saying what Amy said). Alright, so I need to get to the point sooner.

My H1 text is very abstract, "Write CSS as fast as you can sketch."

Most of the feedback I got from the panel was stuff I could implement immediately, like changing contrast, making a few copy edits, etc. (and I'll get to those comments shortly, because they are probably relevant to you). But the biggest takeaway for me, and quite frankly the most annoying, was that everyone had a different opinion on what the benefit they would get if they read my book.
